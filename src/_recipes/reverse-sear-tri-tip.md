    note: to taste
steps:
  - text: Dry brine the tri-tip — coat all over with the salt and rest uncovered in the fridge, ideally several hours or overnight.
    why: Salt needs time to penetrate. A wet surface steams instead of searing; uncovered fridge time dries the exterior so it browns properly later.
  - text: Mix the pepper, garlic powder, smoked paprika, brown sugar, onion powder, and cayenne. Pat the meat dry and rub it on just before cooking.
    why: Salt goes on early to brine; the rest goes on late so the sugar and spices don't draw out moisture or scorch during the long smoke.
  - text: Smoke at 225°F on the GMG until the internal temp hits 120°F, pulling for the carryover ahead.
    why: Low and slow brings the whole roast up evenly, leaving almost no gray band. 120°F gives you room before the sear pushes it to medium-rare.
  - text: Rest the roast while you get the Weber screaming hot — a tight bed of coals, lid off.
    why: A short rest here lets the surface dry again; a wet roast won't take a hard crust.
  - text: Sear hard on all sides until a deep crust forms and the internal reads 130°F. Pull and rest 10 minutes.
    why: 130°F is medium-rare after carryover. The rest lets the juices redistribute so they stay in the meat, not on the board.
  - text: Find the grain — tri-tip has two grain directions that meet at an angle. Slice each section thin, across its own grain.
    why: One straight cut runs with the grain on half the roast and ruins the texture there. Respecting the two-grain structure is the whole game with tri-tip.
---

Pairs with crispy smashed russets and a salsa verde made bright with lime. Leftovers go cold into a chopped salad or warm into tacos — add the meat at the end so it doesn't toughen.
